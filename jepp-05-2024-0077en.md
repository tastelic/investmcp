## **Abstract** 

**Purpose** – The Indonesian Government has high procurement corruption. Implementing electronic procurement poses numerous challenges, as it can still provide opportunities for corruption. This study aims to examine how implementing e-procurement reduces corruption through user perceptions. 

**Design/methodology/approach** – For the analysis, this study applied a mixed-method approach by distributing questionnaires and conducting interviews to procurement actors from ministries, institutions, provincial governments and suppliers of government goods and services. The primary data were analyzed using the structural equation model SmartPLS and NVivo software. 

**Findings** – The empirical results show that the implementation of e-procurement is significant in reducing corruption in the Indonesian Government. 

**Research limitations/implications** – This study did not accommodate local government for more numerous government respondents. 

**Practical implications** – This study highlights that the government assesses the implementation of e-procurement higher than the supplier when suppliers more agree with the existence of procurement corruption than the government. 

**Originality/value** – By employing the technology acceptance model, this study shows how the ease of use and usefulness of e-procurement affect corruption in the procurement process. 

**Keywords** E-Procurement, Technology acceptance model, Corruption, Mixed method, Government **Paper type** Research paper 

## **1. Introduction** 

Public procurement is one of the main concerns of governments worldwide because it involves large amounts of funding. In Indonesia, procurement spending accounts for a significant portion of the government budget (Sikka and Lehman, 2015). Since it involves large amounts of money, tends to have information asymmetry and is associated with the bureaucratic nature of decision making, public procurement is highly vulnerable to fraud and corruption (Jimenez _et al_ ., 2022). In many countries, corruption in public procurement has become a major problem that has financial, environmental, and social impacts and affects innovation and trust in the government (Kang _et al_ ., 2023). The United Nations Convention against Corruption (UNCAC) has reported that most corruption cases are related to public procurement, and losses due to this corruption reach billions of dollars every year (United Nations, 2013). According to Transparency International’s financial report, the effect of corruption on the procurement of goods and services is the use of funds for non-essential purposes with a fairly high budget, thereby producing low state revenues. Inefficiency, ineffectiveness, and corruption in the procurement function result in enormous financial losses. This study aims to examine the effectiveness of e-procurement in reducing corruption in terms of the acceptance or adoption of this system to reduce corruption in the procurement process from government and supplier perspectives. According to previous studies, supplier selection and monitoring (Changalima _et al._ , 2024), strategy and innovation (Karttunen _et al._ , 2022) contribute to public procurement’s efficiency, competency and competitiveness. ## **2. Theory and hypothesis** 

## _2.1 Agency theory_ 

This study uses agency theory that focuses on the problem of corruption in the public procurement process in Indonesia. In this regard, the Indonesian population people are the ultimate principal, and the government acts not only as the agent of the people but also as the principal of the parties involved in public procurement (Almquist _et al._ , 2013; Rustiarini _et al._ , 2019). This theory helps in understanding the risks of corruption in government procurement by considering information asymmetry in the public procurement process that can affect the relationship between the government and procurement practitioners or system users as agents (Khan and Krishnan, 2021). According to Liu (2021), the interests of agents often deviate from those of principals due to the information asymmetry that benefits agents in terms of personal gains. Agency problems occur when procurement practitioners carry out corrupt transactions for their own interests to the detriment of the interests of the principals. To limit agency problems, principals can design monitoring and supervision schemes to curb potential abuse. 

## _2.2 Technology acceptance model_ 

The TAM uses the core assumption that individual use of technology is influenced by factors that determine technological behavior, which can be linked to corrupt behaviors, namely, perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use, both of which have an impact on user attitudes toward the information technology (IT) system (Nandankar and Sachan, 2020). The TAM measures behavioral- or individual-level intentions to use a system (Soong _et al._ , 2020) as a decision maker in technology adoption within an organization (Jones, 2017). TAM links individual recipients’ approval of a technology feature to their behavioral intentions and actual consequences of the use (Autry _et al._ , 2010). The better individuals feel the ease of using the system and its benefits, the better their intention to adopt e-procurement, the more accepted and successful the implementation of the system will be. 

This model has been used in previous studies on the relationship between the use of information communication technology and corruption in several countries (Neupane _et al._ , 2012; Xinli, 2015), whose findings confirm that the level of corruption decreases as users become more adept at using technology. Technology can be accepted if it is useful and easy to use, and the intention and behavior of the users influence the level of corruption (Ntemi and Mbamba, 2016). Moreover, Sambasivan _et al._ (2010) shows that perceived usefulness, ease of use, service assurance and quality, and responsiveness are strongly related to the intention to use e-procurement and to actual user behavior. 

## _2.3 E-procurement_ 

In many countries worldwide, e-procurement has become a key component in the reform and modernization of public procurement frameworks. The use of e-procurement can be extremely efficient at increasing competition and transparency, thus greatly helping to reduce corruption in public procurement (United Nations, 2013). E-procurement can provide access to public tenders, increase competition, and enable the detection of bid rigging cases. In addition, e-procurement brings integrity benefits, as it can limit direct interactions between procurement officials and potential suppliers. E-procurement systems can also help ensure that procurement practitioners have relevant access to increased reach and competition, as well as easily detect irregularities and corruption (Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development [OECD], 2016). 

Specifically in government context, Adebiyi _et al_ . (2010) define e-procurement as the online application of IT and infrastructure for processing, evaluating, and reporting government procurement. According to Bof and Previtali (2010), e-procurement in the public sector is a shared technology term used to automate internal and external processes in ordering goods and services. 

Starting 2012, Indonesia’s ministries, institutions, regional governments and suppliers are required to carry out procurement of goods and services electronically as part of e-government support. The LKPP (Indonesia’s national public procurement agency) organized procurement system called SPSE (electronic procurement system). SPSE has interconnections with information systems for planning, budgeting, payments, asset management and other information systems related to procurement system. The scope of SPSE consists of procurement planning, procurement preparation, supplier selection, contract implementation, work handover, supplier management and electronic catalog. SPSE’s supporting services include the national procurement portal, human resources management for procurement of goods and services, advocacy and resolution of legal problems, community participation, learning resources and monitoring and evaluation.

## _2.4 Corruption_ 

The definition of corruption in Indonesia is explained in the regulation number 31 years 1999. Based on this regulation, types of corruption are grouped into seven criminal acts of corruption, namely loss of state finances, bribery, embezzlement in office, extortion, fraudulent acts, conflicts of interest in procurement, and gratification. In particular, conflict of interest in procurement means a civil servant or state administrator who, either directly or indirectly, deliberately participates in contracting, procurement or rental even though he or she is assigned to manage or supervise it. Corruption in government procurement of goods and services occurs due to conflicts of interest in the process, such as actions carried out by state officials who deliberately participate in the procurement process. This can influence wrong and subjective decision making, adverse selection, and prioritizing personal interests, which ultimately harms the country (KPK, 2021). 

The corruption of public procurement occurs during procurement. The handbook curbing corruption in public procurement from Transparency International (2006) states that corruption exists in the phases of assessment, preparation, contractor and award, and contract implementation. According to the Guidebook on anti-corruption in public procurement and prevention of corruption in public procurement (OECD, 2016), corruption exists in three procurement stages: pre-tender, tender, and post-tender. Meanwhile (Transparency International, 2014), in Practical Guide Curbing Corruption in Public Procurement, mentioned that corruption happens in procurement planning, bidding, bid evaluation, contract implementation, monitoring, and evaluation. Peltier-Rivest (2018) states that potential fraud in procurement takes many forms, such as corruption, collusion, and tender fixation, and is found in various phases from budgeting to utilization. Meanwhile, the Indonesia Procurement Watch (IPW) published an anti-corruption toolkit for government procurement of good and services (IPW, 2008). There are ten forms of

## _2.5 Hypothesis development_ 

The successful implementation of technology and information systems is demonstrated by their adoption, utilization, and performance (Xu and Lu, 2022). In this regard, the acceptance of the e-procurement system at the organizational and individual levels is reflected in the intentions and behaviors of its users (Albano _et al._ , 2015), which are affected by the system’s perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use. The acceptance of procurement practitioners has a great impact on the perceptions of corruption or irregularities that occur at all stages of the procurement process. In other words, the implementation of e-procurement, measured by its usefulness and ease of use, will increase user acceptance and attitudes to reduce corruption perceptions in the procurement process. The perceptions of procurement actors are related to their behavior and intentions and affect corruption in all procurement phases. In other words, user perceptions will influence corrupt behaviors. 

The results of previous studies mostly indicate that e-procurement systems have an effect on the prevention and reduction of corruption. The main benefit of implementing e-procurement lies in its potential to reduce the discretion of public procurement officials, which is detrimental to the procurement process, i.e. through moral hazard, short-term opportunistic behavior, and adverse selection problems (M�elon and Spruk, 2020). E-procurement systems are interconnected and have a positive and significant effect on the prevention of fraud and corruption (Neupane _et al._ , 2012; Wicaksono _et al._ , 2017) by reducing moral hazards, adverse selection, and information asymmetry (Guida _et al._ , 2023). This finding is in line with the results of another prior study conducted in a city in Indonesia that also showed that the implementation of e-procurement has a significant effect on preventing fraud in the procurement of goods and services (Utami and Indrijawati, 2020). With its numerous benefits, the implementation of e-procurement is expected to reduce procurement problems, findings, and corruption in ministries, institutions, and provincial governments (Choi _et al._ , 2016). The user of the system will experience the ease of use and usefulness of the e-procurement that will reflect their intention and behavior to do corruption. Based on this, this study hypothesizes that implementing e-procurement will reduce corruption. This hypothesis tests the negative effect of e-procurement on corruption. ## **3. Methodology** 

## _3.1 Research procedure_ 

This study applied an explanatory sequential design, a mixed-method design where the quantitative study was conducted first and the qualitative study was designed to follow up and explain the quantitative study. Therefore, this study places more emphasis on quantitative methods than qualitative methods. Moreover, a quantitative study identifies the results of a quantitative study that requires better understanding and further explanation (Creswell, 2011); responses obtained from interviews enrich the interpretation of the quantitative analysis results (Clark, 2010). In other words, the results of qualitative studies are needed to expand or strengthen the results of quantitative studies. 

This study started by preparing the first draft of the questionnaire, followed by conducting Focus Group Discussion (FGD) and compiling the second draft of the questionnaire. Next, pilot tests 1 and 2 were carried out for validity and reliability using Statistical Product and Service Solutions (SPSS) until the questionnaires were finalized and ready to be distributed. This study followed the procedure of the mixed-methods study by Creswell (2010). First, the quantitative data were collected and analyzed. The qualitative study was designed by identifying the results of the quantitative study, preparing the interview questions, and selecting the samples. The next step was to collect qualitative data via interviews for qualitative analysis. Finally, the results of both studies were integrated to draw conclusions. 

To test the hypothesis, the independent variable in this study is the e-procurement system, which is measured using the dimensions of usefulness and ease of use. The e-procurement indicators are determined according to previous studies of Neupane _et al._ (2012) who adopt the TAM theory. The dependent variable is corruption in the public procurement process, measured using the dimensions of planning, preparation, and implementation phases based on the perceptions of system users. The corruption indicators are determined according to local and global guidelines, for example from IPW, BPKP, OECD and Transparency International. All indicators are agreed with FGD result. ## **4. Results and discussion** 

## _4.1 Descriptive analysis_ 

This study received 327 respondents from 91 participating government offices in Indonesia— 30 ministries, 33 government institutions, and 28 provincial governments—and 697 respondents from 653 supplier companies—for a total of 744 units of analysis. The majority of respondents were aged between 30 and 50 years and had a bachelor’s degree. Among the government respondents, 74% worked in the procurement units, and the remaining 26% were from the general bureaus of ministries, institutions, and provincial governments. Most of the respondents were from the selection working group and procurement officials. For the supplier respondents, 68% were company owners. Based on the type of procurement, 36% of the suppliers provided goods for the government, 19% provided other services, and 11% were construction companies. 

The results of the descriptive analysis reveal that the majority of respondents agree with the adoption of e-procurement by the Indonesian Government because of its ease of use and usefulness. Moreover, with regard to corruption, the respondents disagree with the occurrence of corruption at all procurement stages. Table 3 presents the descriptions of e-procurement and corruption. 

Based on respondent data, men aged over 50 with postgraduate education rate the highest implementation of e-procurement. According to government respondents, the procurement unit and procurement official positions agreed most with the implementation of e-procurement. From supplier respondents, the supplier of goods and consultancy services, and staff positions are most agreed with the implementation of e-procurement. ## _4.3 Qualitative analysis_ 

_4.3.1 Interview design._ The study conduct mixed method with explanatory or follow up sequential method. It means, qualitative data collection and analysis will follow up the quantitative results. The results of quantitative analysis show the implementation of e-procurement is significant in reducing corruption. However, the results require additional explanation. Firstly, there are differences perception result between group of government and supplier. The government assesses the implementation of e-procurement better than suppliers. Meanwhile, suppliers assess corruption more often in the procurement process than the government. Second, the use of procurement system or applications provided by the government is low. Third, although the hypothesis result is significant, the R-square is unexpectedly low. Fourth, there are differences results between descriptive and measurement for the e-procurement and corruption dimensions. With the identification above, an interview process must be conducted to confirm and interpret the quantitative results. Interview session explore opinions, challenges, and suggestions from qualified sources to enrich the results of this study (Creswell, 2011). 

The interviews were conducted with nine qualified people, consisting of one person from the Ministry (SS1), five persons from government institutions (SS2-SS6), one person from provincial governments (SS7), and two persons from suppliers (SS8-SS9). All interviews were conducted in the source speaker’s office, with approximately one hour allocated for each interview. NVivo software was used to group and determine the codes or themes according to the transcripts. 

_4.3.2 Interview results._ There are eight interview questions that were developed based on the results of the hypothesis testing and measurement evaluations. Table 8 presents the results of the interviews and the sample of quotes or transcripts. 

The first question asks how the implementation of e-procurement has an impact on reducing corruption. The source speakers agree that e-procurement can make the procurement process more transparent, open, and accessible. It requires less interaction with others. In addition, by using the system, the procurement process can be better evaluate and monitor. 

The next question concerns the challenges of implementing e-procurement. The source speakers agree that the implementation of e-procurement in the Indonesian Government is not optimal, sometimes unreliable and manual, not in accordance with procurement needs, not responsive to user complaints or queries and not accessible by user. This is caused by a lack of integration within procurement applications, lack of competition among suppliers and qualified procurement officials and also the changes in procurement regulation. 

The third question concerns the occurrence of procurement corruption at different stages of the procurement process. The source speakers agree that corruption occurs frequently at the preparation and planning stage, as there are activities of selecting suppliers and procurement officers, determining price offerings, and choosing specifications for goods and services. However, corruption also occurs during the implementation stage when goods and services are delivered. 

The fourth question asks about the usefulness and ease of use of implementing e-procurement. The source speakers agreed the convenience of e-procurement that can speed up the procurement process, reduce time, and minimize cost and documentation. They believe that both ease of use and usefulness are important in adopting e-procurement.

## **5. Discussion** 

The study results that e-procurement can reduce corruption. It’s supported by higher education of respondent profile that most agreed with the implementation of e-procurement and least agreed with the occurrence of procurement corruption in Indonesia government. The finding as shown in Tables 5 and 6 are in accordance with the results of a study by Nandankar and Sachan (2020) that perceived usefulness and ease of use are important drivers of 

e-procurement adoption, utilization, and performance, which have an impact on attitudes toward the use of IT systems. The interview result also agrees with the quantitative result that the e-procurement system is both useful and easy to use. Although usefulness is more related to e-procurement, ease of use is also important and need to be supported by the speed of the system. According to Chen _et al._ (2002), perceived benefits are a significant factor that influences technology adoption attitudes. Technology can be accepted as long as its usefulness is perceived, it is easy to use, or there is behavior in using the system (Ntemi and Mbamba, 2016). In this regard, Neupane _et al._ (2012) supports the idea that the intention to use e-procurement in the public sector is significantly related to the concepts of ease of use and usefulness in developing countries.

## **6. Conclusion and recommendation** 

This study answers the questions of how e-procurement has been implemented and whether it can reduce procurement corruption in the Indonesian Government. The results of this study prove that the implementation of e-procurement, as measured by the dimensions of ease of use and usefulness of the system, significantly affects corruption in the procurement process. The results of the qualitative study with interviews confirm the results of the hypothesis testing. Therefore, the use of a mixed-methods design provides more comprehensive analysis than the use of quantitative analysis only. From the user’s perspective, this study shows that government asses the implementation of e-procurement higher than supplier when supplier more agreed with the existence of procurement corruption than government. The study also proves that many public procurement systems are not effectively applied by both users. 

Theoretically, this study shows the implementation or measurement of e-procurement using TAM based on its ease of use and usefulness. This study, therefore, contributes to the correlation between the TAM and agency theory where the ease of use and usefulness of e-procurement can reduce information asymmetry, moral hazard and procurement corruption in protecting the public interest. This study also proves that the TAM as part of the theory of planned behavior is correlated with the perception of corruption. 

This study provides practical input and suggestions for the government on how to better implement e-procurement. The system’s accessibility, integration, and responsiveness need to be improved. The study also suggests increasing the qualifications of procurement actors related to their integrity and capacity in performing the procurement process. The result also suggests more competition and evaluation in choosing suppliers through the system with less government intervention. In addition, the government needs to conduct better monitoring and control throughout the system and make extra efforts to reduce corruption during the procurement preparation phase. This recommendation and type of research can be followed by other countries or researcher who face similar situation to effectively use e-procurement in combating corruption. As a developing country in the Asia–Pacific region, Indonesia can also learn more from the successful anti-corruption models of Singapore and Hong Kong that have better index in their perception of corruption to address the challenges. 

This study has several limitations that suggest further studies on this topic. First, future studies can consider relating e-procurement to other variables, such as procurement governance, that may have a greater impact on reducing corruption. In addition, further studies could involve not only provincial government respondents but also local government respondents; thus, these respondents could be more numerous and represent all of Indonesia’s governments.
