## 1. Introduction
Context and Importance
Corruption in local government investment is a persistent challenge in Indonesia, undermining public trust,
deterring foreign and domestic investment, and impeding equitable development. The post-2001
decentralization reforms, while intended to empower local authorities and improve service delivery, have
inadvertently created new opportunities for corruption by dispersing fiscal and administrative authority to local
governments with varying capacities and oversight mechanisms 1 25. The complexity of local political
coalitions, weak internal controls, and the slow adoption of anti-corruption technologies such as e-procurement
further exacerbate these risks. Addressing corruption in local investment is thus critical for Indonesia’s
sustainable development, investment climate, and the realization of effective, accountable governance.
## 2. Theoretical Frameworks
Decentralization and Corruption Opportunities
Decentralization in Indonesia has shifted significant fiscal and administrative powers to local governments.
While this was intended to foster local autonomy and responsiveness, it has also fragmented oversight and

Resource Curse Dynamics: In resource-rich regions, fiscal decentralization can amplify corruption risks, as

Internal Control Systems: The effectiveness of internal controls (e.g., COSO framework components) is

Political Coalitions and Legislative Dynamics
Local political structures—especially the size and composition of local legislatures and coalition dynamics—
play a decisive role in shaping corruption risks:
Coalition Size and Complexity: Larger legislatures and complex coalitions are associated with higher
corruption, as more actors seek rents and oversight becomes diluted 5 30.
Party Composition: Districts dominated by secular parties tend to have higher corruption than those with
Islamic party representation, suggesting that party ideology and coalition structure matter 30.
Veto Players: More veto players (i.e., actors whose agreement is necessary for decisions) can increase
corruption by creating more opportunities for bribery and collusion 31.
Internal Audit and Oversight Weaknesses
Audit Capability and Maturity: Strong internal audit functions reduce non-compliance and corruption, but
are often undermined by political interference, lack of resources, and insufficient training 4 32 33 34.
Fraud Prevention Mechanisms: Integration of risk-based audits, fraud prevention, and external oversight is
essential for effective governance 4 35.
Financial Manipulation and Accrual Deviations
Discretionary Accruals: Manipulation of financial statements through discretionary accruals is a common
method for concealing corruption in local investment projects 15 16 17 36.
Political-Budgetary Cycles: Investment budgets and accrual manipulation often spike around election
periods, reflecting political incentives for rent-seeking 37.
Complex Licensing and Investment Procedures
Licensing Complexity: Overlapping and opaque licensing procedures facilitate bribery and rent-seeking;
reforms such as online risk-based licensing systems show promise but are not yet widespread 38 39.
## Synthesis
The theoretical frameworks highlight that corruption in local government investment is a product of
institutional design (decentralization), political dynamics (coalitions, veto players), and administrative capacity
(internal controls, audit maturity). Effective prevention requires integrated approaches that address these
interlocking vulnerabilities.

## 5. Real-world Implications
Policy and Practice
Strengthen Internal Controls: Prioritize audit capability, independence, and maturity at the local level.
Promote E-Procurement: Invest in ICT infrastructure, training, and change management to overcome
adoption barriers.
Leverage Community Participation: Institutionalize community-based oversight, especially in regions with
strong customary law traditions.
Legal and Regulatory Reform: Clarify regulations, strengthen enforcement, and protect whistleblowers.
Foster Inter-Agency Coordination: Build trust and integrated approaches among anti-corruption agencies.
Address Political Dynamics: Reform local political structures to reduce coalition complexity and veto
player proliferation.
Risks of Inaction
Continued high levels of corruption in local investment.
Erosion of public trust and deterrence of investment.
Inefficient allocation of public resources and persistent development gaps.
