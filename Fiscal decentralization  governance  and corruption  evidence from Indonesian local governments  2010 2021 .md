## **ABSTRACT** 

the purpose of this research is to explore the interaction between fiscal decentralization and governance, which jointly affect the level of corruption in local governments in indonesia. By utilizing panel data from 548 district/municipal governments during the period from 2010 to 2021, this research aims to examine the impact of local own source revenue (losr), Balance Funds, and budget efficiency on the level of corruption. governance is captured by audit reports of internal control weaknesses. By utilizing panel regression, this research explores the direct and moderating effects of governance. the results indicate that losr and budget efficiency are negatively related to the level of corruption, whereas Balance Funds are positively related to the level of corruption. governance moderates the anti-corruption impact of budget efficiency but surprisingly exacerbates the pro-corruption impact of losr and Balance Funds. Further robustness checks indicate the existence of different misuse vulnerabilities for general Allocation Funds (gAF) and special Allocation Funds (sAF). this research extends the existing literature on fiscal decentralization by exploring the interaction between different fiscal decentralization instruments and the level of corruption.

## **1. Introduction** 

Fiscal decentralization has become a central component of public sector reforms in many countries, especially in developing economies with the aim of increasing the efficiency of the government (Afonso et al., 2024). Fiscal decentralization aims to enhance local government accountability and improve the efficiency of public resource allocation, the idea of decentralization of the fiscal role of the government to local governments has gained prominence (Faguet, 2014; oates, 1999). however, decentralization can also result in negative outcomes if the local government institutions lack the ability to govern. in this context, decentralization can create opportunities for rent-seeking activities (prud’homme, 1995). 

the quality of governance plays a critical role in assessing of the link between the decentralization of the financial system and the level of accountability or the potential for corruption (saputra & setiawan, 2021). For instance, effective governance can be a critical factor in ensuring that the decentralized financial system does not result in corruption. on the contrary, a weak governance system can provide opportunities for corruption to thrive in a decentralized financial system (hamidi, 2024). thus, the link between governance quality and the decentralized financial system must be examined to understand the conditions under which decentralization can be effective in the fight against corruption (north, 1990; treisman, 2007).

## _**2.1. Agency theory and collective action theory**_ 

Agency theory explains the contract between a principal and an agent. the theory was initially used in the context of business organizations (Jensen & Meckling, 1976). however, it is also used in the public sector with the citizen as the principal and the government as the agent in the provision of public services (Zimmerman, 1977). in indonesia, the relationship between the government and the people is

institutionalized in the context of regional autonomy and fiscal balance through laws no. 32 and no. 33 of 2004. in an ideal situation, the government is expected to act in the interest of the people. however, in the presence of information asymmetry, the government agent can act in his or her own self-interest and indulge in corrupt practices (groenendijk, 1997). in the initial applications of the theory, corruption is explained because of the incentive incompatibility and information asymmetry between the principals and the agents (persson et al., 2013). 

Agency theory is not without limitations. the major limitation is the tendency of the theory to explain corruption as an individual-level phenomenon. however, subsequent applications of the theory have explained that corruption is often embedded in the institutional context (Mungiu-pippidi, 2015). in the context of collective action, individuals may engage in corrupt practices because it is the expected behavior in the organization. this phenomenon may be explained through the prisoner’s dilemma (Kuhn & pardos-prado, 2021). in the context of the prisoner’s dilemma, individuals may engage in corrupt practices because it is the expected behavior in the organization. however, at the same time, they know the ill effects of the behavior. the rules and regulations may not be effective in the context of the organization because the corrupt behavior is a collective phenomenon. the agency theory and the collective action theory of corruption may be used in conjunction with each other. the agency theory explains the individual-level factors leading to corrupt behavior. the collective action theory of corruption may be used to explain the institutional factors. 

## _**2.2. Fiscal decentralization**_ 

Fiscal decentralization is an integral component of indonesia’s governance reform, intended to build the effectiveness of local governments by delegating financial authority from the central government to subnational governments (saputra & setiawan, 2021). this is expected to improve service delivery, build greater accountability, and more responsive policymaking by subnational governments, owing to their proximity with the people. however, empirical findings show a mixed result on the effects of fiscal decentralization on corruption (Dao & hong, 2025; nirola et al., 2022). on one hand, decentralization can reduce the level of corruption by building greater monitoring and accountability; on the other hand, it can exacerbate it, especially in regions with weak governance. this largely depends on the level of political competition, as well as the quality of governance in a country. 

Fiscal decentralization in indonesia began in 2001 as a component of post-authoritarian reform, with a boost from the conduct of local elections in 2005. the legal bases for fiscal decentralization in indonesia are law no. 32/2004 on regional Autonomy and law no. 33/2004 on Fiscal Balance. Although there is a substantial increase in the allocation of resources to local governments, the results in building better governance and fighting corruption are less encouraging. the concept of local fiscal capacity is measured by the local own-source revenue (losr), showing the level of fiscal independence. nevertheless, significant differences are observed among the regions; some are still heavily reliant on central government transfers. this is attributed to the differences in the capacity of the administration and the effectiveness of regulations and tax collection. past studies also show that the structure and policies of local finance may create room for opportunistic practices and corruption (cifuentes-Faura et al., 2023). to address the differences in the regions, the central government provides the Balance Funds. these include the general Allocation Fund (gAF), special Allocation Fund (sAF), and revenue sharing Fund (rsF). the objective is to achieve fiscal equalization while maintaining the development priorities at the national level. 

lastly, the performance of decentralization is also measured using financial ratios. this is done to evaluate the efficiency and fiscal discipline. With the support of regulatory acts such as law no. 23/2014 and law no. 17/2003, this provides a framework for analyzing the effectiveness of decentralization in terms of more effective and accountable financial management.

the importance of governance has been extensively emphasized in international policy discourse, including those conducted under the aegis of the organization for economic co-operation and Development (oecD), wherein transparency and accountability are considered essential features of effective governments (cuadrado-Ballesteros, 2014). empirical studies also show that governance plays a critical role in policy effectiveness, public accountability, and control of corruption, particularly in decentralized systems (cifuentes-Faura, 2024; nguyen et al., 2020). the effectiveness of governance varies between systems; it is influenced by factors such as institutional capacities, rules, and socio-cultural factors. Weak governance is often associated with inefficiencies, suboptimal public service delivery, and high risks of corruption (Avelino et al., 2014). to overcome such challenges, governance has become an integral part of new public Management (npM), which places strong emphasis on effective budgeting, accounting, and transparency in public sector institutions (ribeiro et al., 2013).

## _**2.4. Corruption**_ 

the anti-corruption system in indonesia is embedded in law number 31 of 1999, which describes corruption as an illegal act to achieve personal enrichment at the expense of state finances or the economy. corruption is a global problem that remains a topical challenge to nations around the world, affecting their development and growth (Alterman, 2019; un, 2022). 

corruption has a detrimental impact on economic growth and government effectiveness due to distortions in resource allocation, which affect government programs (Andersen, 2009; Mungiu-pippidi, 2015). corruption is also an essential factor in poor governance and development underperformance (grindle, 2007). in the context of public sector institutions, corruption often manifests as a misuse of resources, particularly in a decentralized financial system (Alfano et al., 2019). 

the risks of corruption in indonesia have risen in parallel with the implementation of decentralization reforms since the reformasi period. Despite the government’s efforts to strengthen local government through law number 32 of 2004, which has been reinforced through law number 23 of 2014, there has been an increase in new avenues of corruption through decentralization. empirical studies conducted in various regions have shown that financial management has contributed to local-level corruption (Basri & nabiha, 2014; Maria & gudono, 2017). 

Audits carried out by the Audit Board of indonesia (BpK-ri) often show discrepancies in local government financial management, which sometimes leads to legal action taken by the corruption eradication commission (KpK) (gani & Kristanto, 2013; Maria et al., 2021). this is often attributed to information asymmetry between local governments and central governments, bureaucratic complexities, as well as political pressures during financial planning (Maria & gudono, 2017). corruption risks also tend to arise in local governments facing financial challenges such as budget deficits (oto-peralías et al., 2013). 

## **3. Research hypothesis** 

## _**3.1. The relationship of fiscal decentralization and corruption**_ 

regional independence refers to the ability of local governments to operate without relying on financial aid from the central government. in indonesia, this independence is ideally reflected when regional expenditures are fully funded by local own source revenue (losr) (ritonga et al., 2012). From an agency theory perspective, government officials may engage in corrupt acts driven by personal gain

motives (groenendijk, 1997). the growing independence of local governments signals an increase in economic capability and community wealth, which in turn raises losr, a resource vulnerable to corruption. 

empirical studies support the link between losr and corruption. using data from 64 countries, lessmann and Markwardt (2010) demonstrate that decentralization acceptance correlates with a lower score on the country risk guide, indicating more corruption cases. similarly, Altunbaş and thornton (2012) find that state revenue from regional taxes significantly lowers the corruption perception index (cpi) score, implying increased corruption. research using alternative corruption measures confirms these findings: shon and cho (2020) show that fiscal revenues in the united states correlate positively with corruption cases. in indonesia, losr significantly impacts both the incidence of corruption and the resulting state financial losses (Maria et al., 2021; ratmono et al., 2021). Based on the theoretical arguments discussed above, this study formulates the following testable hypotheses: 

**H** 1a **:** local own source revenue has a positive effect on corruption in local government. 

Balance Funds are aid allocations from the central government intended to support full decentralization (Maria et al., 2019). however, the approval process for balance funds is politically charged (Maria & gudono, 2017), as outlined in government regulation pp no. 55 of 2005. this political dynamic creates information asymmetry and opportunities for corruption. During fund submission, political lobbying plays a decisive role in approval, reflecting agency theory’s conflict between principals and agents. corruption related to balance funds often arises from allocations influenced by personal or group interests, a fact frequently reported by government auditors (Maria & gudono, 2017). 

studies confirm balance funds as a key factor influencing regional corruption. research encompassing all indonesian local governments reveals that balance funds increase the likelihood of corruption (Maria et al., 2019) and contribute to significant state losses from corrupt practices (ratmono et al., 2021). thus, the following hypothesis is formulated: 

**H** 1b **:** Balance Funds have a positive effect on corruption in local government. 

Budget pressure is a critical challenge in decentralization, reflecting when local governments cannot meet budget allocations set by the central government (Kloha et al., 2005). this pressure signals the region’s inability to provide quality services to its community (Jones & Walker, 2007). Budget deficits hinder innovation and regional progress, and budget pressure is often quantified using financial efficiency ratios. 

Budget constraints may signal potential corruption risks. According to agency theory, regional officials (agents) face pressure to demonstrate good performance despite financial difficulties, which can incentivize corrupt behavior. in indonesia, corruption tendencies persist in regions facing budget constraints (Maria & gudono, 2017). Although regulations allow local governments to revise budgets via loans (pp no. 30, 2011), these revisions involve complex political procedures, opening loopholes for corruption (isaksen, 2005). efficient budget management, as reflected by favorable financial ratios, reduces budget pressure and thus corruption opportunities (Wu & heerink, 2016). 

cross-country studies support this relationship. Mauro (1998) finds that effective decentralized government spending reduces corruption based on cpi data across 100 countries. likewise, lessmann and Markwardt (2010) show government spending efficiency lowers corruption risk according to the international country risk guide (icrg). Ko and Zhi (2013) report similar findings in china, where efficient government spending reduced corruption cases between 1998 and 2008. in indonesia, Aswar et al. (2022) confirm that local government spending effectiveness significantly reduces corruption incidence. hence, the following hypothesis is proposed: 

**H** 1c **:** Budget efficiency has a negative effect on corruption in local government. 

## _**3.2. Governance as a moderating variable**_ 

treating governance as a moderating variable rather than a mediator or direct determinant is grounded in both theoretical logic and empirical patterns documented in the decentralization and corruption literature. conceptually, governance represents the quality of institutional arrangements, including rule of 

6 s. B. KristAnto et Al. 

law, bureaucratic integrity, transparency, and accountability mechanisms, that shape how other policy interventions operate. in this sense, governance functions as an enabling or constraining condition, not as a sequential outcome of decentralization nor as an independent driver acting in isolation. 

From a theoretical standpoint, decentralization is expected to reduce corruption only under certain institutional conditions. the fiscal federalism literature (Faguet, 2014; oates, 1999) and the institutional economics tradition (north, 1990) argue that the impact of decentralization is contingent upon the institutional environment in which local governments operate. When governance quality is strong, characterized by effective oversight systems, public participation, and credible sanctions, decentralization creates incentives for competition, responsiveness, and improved service delivery, which collectively reduce corruption. conversely, when governance is weak, decentralization may amplify elite capture, rent-seeking, or local collusion, thereby increasing corruption. 

this conditional effect is aligned with contingency theory, which posits that the effectiveness of a policy depends on contextual factors rather than linear cause–effect relationships. As such, governance is more appropriately conceptualized as a contextual moderator that shapes the direction and magnitude of the decentralization, corruption relationship. 

statistically, treating governance as a mediator would imply that decentralization directly improves governance, which then reduces corruption. however, this assumption does not align with institutional theory or empirical evidence in emerging economies, where governance quality is relatively stable, slow-changing, and structurally embedded. similarly, using governance as a direct predictor of corruption would ignore the substantial theoretical emphasis on interaction effects, which suggest that governance strengthens or weakens the influence of fiscal decentralization. 

therefore, positioning governance as a moderating variable provides a more theoretically coherent and empirically consistent model, capturing the conditional institutional mechanisms through which decentralization influences corruption outcomes. Based on the above theoretical reasoning, this study proposes the following hypothesis: 

_H2a: Governance weakens the relationship between Local Own Source Revenue and Corruption in Local Government_ 

_H2b: Governance weakens the relation of Balance Funds and Corruption in Local Government_ 

_H2c: Governance strengthens the negative relationship between Budget Efficiency and Corruption in Local Government_

## **4. Method** 

## _**4.1. Data and data sources**_ 

this research uses panel data from 548 district and municipal governments in indonesia, covering the period from 2010 to 2021. the data was obtained from various official sources. Fiscal decentralization indicators such as local own source revenue (losr), Balance Funds, and budget information were obtained from the Directorate general of Fiscal Balance, Ministry of Finance. governance indicators were constructed based on audit reports published by the supreme Audit Board of indonesia, which provide information on internal control weaknesses identified during government audit reports. Additional fiscal information was obtained from the financial reports published by local governments. 

## _**4.2. Population and sample**_ 

the population in this study is all components of the government in indonesia. While the sample to be used is the local government. Based on data from the Directorate general of regional Autonomy, indonesia currently has 548 local governments. From this sample, the researcher will divide the analysis into two parts. the first part consists of the level i local government, which contains 34 provinces. While the second part consists of the level ii local government, which contains 416 regencies and 98 Municipalities. the time span of the research years to be taken starts from 2010 to 2021. For the 2022 local government Financial report, it is currently not fully available at the BpK-ri and is still in the audit process. 

## _**4.3. Variable definitions**_ 

## _**4.3.1. Corruption (COR)**_ 

the corruption eradication commission (KpK) identifies three indicators commonly used to assess corruption risks: the integrity Assessment survey (spi), the corruption perception index (cpi), and the Anti-corruption perception index (ipAK) (Aclc-KpK, 2022). spi provides an institutional integrity assessment, but it has only been implemented since 2019 and therefore lacks the longitudinal depth required for panel analysis. cpi, although internationally recognized, measures corruption at the national level and cannot capture variation among indonesia’s local governments. Meanwhile, ipAK captures societal anti-corruption attitudes rather than corruption within government institutions. thus, none of these indicators directly capture corruption practices at the local government level. 

Because of these limitations, prior international studies frequently rely on audited financial loss estimates as a more objective proxy for corruption. supreme audit institutions are widely recognized as credible sources for quantifying corruption-related financial irregularities (Ferraz & Finan, 2008; isaksen, 2005). in indonesia, audit findings and loss estimates published by BpK-ri have been used extensively as a validated corruption proxy (isaksen, 2005; ratmono & Darsono, 2022). compared with the number of detected or prosecuted corruption cases, financial loss estimates better reflect the severity of corruption rather than merely its frequency, an insight emphasized in comparative corruption studies (Fan et al., 2009; olken & pande, 2012). Moreover, BpK conducts audits using standardized national procedures, providing a consistent and replicable measure across local governments and over time. 

concerns about measurement error and undetected corruption are inherent to all corruption indicators, including perception-based indices and case counts (svensson, 2005; treisman, 2007). to mitigate these concerns, this study adopts several robustness-oriented strategies. First, we rely exclusively on audited and publicly disclosed BpK-ri loss estimates, which are produced through systematic and legally mandated audit standards, reducing subjectivity and increasing comparability. second, we apply the

natural logarithm to the estimated loss values to stabilize variance, minimize extreme-value distortions, and approximate a normal distribution, an approach commonly recommended in corruption measurement (olken & pande, 2012). third, although financial loss estimates may not capture all forms of corruption, they remain the most direct and institutionally grounded proxy for quantifying local government corruption within indonesia’s decentralized governance structure. this is consistent with international literature showing that audit-based corruption measures outperform detection-based metrics in accuracy and policy relevance (Ferraz & Finan, 2008). 

## _**4.3.2. Local Own Source Revenue (LOSR)**_ 

the first dimension of fiscal decentralization in this study is regional independence. one of the benchmarks for the success of fiscal decentralization is the even distribution of losr in provinces, regencies, and Municipalities in indonesia (ulfa & Alvaro, 2021). in this study, researchers will also use losr as did previous researchers such as Altunbaş & thornton (2012) and changwony & paterson (2019). in its operationalization, this study uses the natural logarithm of the losr value received by the local government. 

## _**4.3.3. Balance funds (BF)**_ 

the second dimension for measuring fiscal decentralization is regional dependence, which is measured by the size of the balance. fund. the balance funds consists of three components, namely general Allocation Fund (gAF), special Allocation Fund (sAF), and revenue sharing Fund (rsF), according to applicable provisions (pp no.55, 2005). For all local governments have gAF components. however, not all regions have sAF and rsF components. sAF is more directed at local governments that have national priority programs. While rsF contains elements of natural resources (sDA) which not all local governments have a balanced amount. For testing in data analysis, this study uses the natural logarithm of the total value of gAF, sAF, and rsF for each local government. 

## _**4.3.4. Budget efficiency (BE)**_ 

the third dimension for measuring fiscal decentralization in this study is the budget. efficiency. use of budget size this efficiency is in accordance with the principle _of money follow function_ (uu no.33, 2004). Budget efficiency is the ratio of the comparison between the amount of spending divided by income. researchers will use this ratio as a measure to represent the dimensions in fiscal decentralization. this ratio will reflect the level of efficiency of budget use by the local government. the smaller the ratio produced, the more efficient the local government is in managing the budget. the use of this measure has been adjusted to previous studies which have proven to be consistent in research Agyemang-Duah et al., (2018) and Arif & Ahmad (2020). 

## _**4.3.5. Governance of government (G)**_ 

in indonesia’s general guidelines for public sector governance (pug-spi), governance emphasizes accountability, transparency, and openness, with BpK-ri audit findings on internal control weaknesses serving as the primary measure due to their alignment with fiscal reporting standards (KnKg, 2022; ratmono & Darsono, 2022). this count inversely reflects governance quality, as higher weaknesses indicate poorer institutional capacity (nguyen et al., 2020). Although alternatives like BsKDn’s local government governance index (itKpD) are under development since 2021, with limited provincial trials and no regency/municipal data (Antaranews, 2023; Media indonesia, 2021), and prior indices lack consistency or recency (Amyulianthy et al., 2022; Maria & halim, 2021; setyaningrum et al., 2017), BpK-ri findings remain the most reliable, replicable proxy for this study. 

the governance level can be defined as the number of audit findings reported by the indonesian supreme Audit Board (BpK). this implies that the higher the number of findings, the lower the level of governance. therefore, the level of governance will be multiplied by −1 to facilitate the interpretation of the results. this will ensure that the coefficient for the governance level reflects the positive relationship between governance level and corruption. this does not alter the properties of the data but merely reverses the sign to ensure that the coefficient reflects the positive relationship between the governance level and corruption.

## _**4.3.6. Control variables**_ 

in this study, researchers used five control variables to test the stability of the data in the analysis. the first control variable is (pop). A large population is a challenge for the local government to manage institutions more effectively. in several studies, the large population will help the monitoring and administration mechanisms for mitigating corruption (Fisman & gatti, 2002; treisman, 2000). on the other hand, a large population also allows regions to achieve high human development potential. this is the impact of increasing economic scale, urbanization, and access to public services (Bloom et al., 2008; pradhan & ravallion, 2000). to measure the population variable, researchers used the natural logarithm of the population in each local government. 

the second control variable is the number of civil servants (cs). Based on the research results goel & nelson (2010), local governments that have many cs will have many gaps _in rent seeking_ and high corruption. _Rent seeking_ itself is an effort to increase personal wealth through _public resources._ the following study provides evidence that the number of cs creates vulnerable points for corruption (Brunetti & Weder, 2003; goel & nelson, 1998; Mauro, 1995; shleifer & vishny, 1993). to measure the variable of the number of cs, the researcher uses the natural logarithm of the number of cs in each local government. 

the third control variable is gross regional Domestic product (grDp). grDp reflects economic health in a region. therefore, regions with high grDp will have a low tendency for corruption, and vice versa (Mauro, 1995; treisman, 2000). to measure the grDp variable, researchers used the natural logarithm of grDp in each local government. 

the fourth control variable is the autonomous age (Age). this variable will see how long the local government has been an autonomous region. regions that have been autonomous for a long time have a low tendency for corruption (Arikan, 2004; Fisman & gatti, 2002; lessmann & Markwardt, 2010). to measure the Age variable, it will be calculated from the age of the local government since it was determined to be an autonomous region. 

the fifth control variable is the sample area group (JAvA). researchers divide it into Java and non-Java islands based on observations in previous studies. the results of the study show that the local government in Java island is more controlled in its management so that the level of corruption is more controlled (Amyulianthy et al., 2022; Arifin et al., 2015; Furqan et al., 2020; Maria & gudono, 2017; ratmono & Darsono, 2022; setyaningrum et al., 2017; shoesmith et al., 2020). to measure this variable, researchers use _dummy_ 1 if the province, regency, or Municipal is on the island of Java. ## _**5.4.1. The impact of fiscal decentralization on corruption**_ 

Fiscal decentralization, in this context, is seen as one of the institutional changes aimed at increasing efficiency in service delivery by giving local governments more fiscal power (Kyriacou & roca-sagalés, 2011). Decentralization is expected to bring about greater innovation, economic potential, and administrative efficiency in local governments (Baskaran, 2011). in indonesia, decentralization is institutionalized in the country’s decentralization policy, as outlined in law no. 32 of 2004 and law no. 33 of 2004, aimed at increasing political, administrative, and fiscal autonomy in regional governance. however, decentralization in governance could also lead to governance challenges, especially when fiscal decentralization is faster than institutional decentralization. From an agency theory perspective, decentralization could bring about opportunities for opportunistic behavior, especially when there is a lack of effective monitoring, as well as information asymmetry between central and local governments (groenendijk, 1997). 

in this paper, fiscal decentralization is explored along three axes: regional fiscal independence, regional fiscal dependency, and budget efficiency. regional fiscal independence is used as an indicator by local own source revenue (losr); that is, the capacity of the local government to finance itself. regional dependency is used as an indicator by balancing the funds received from the central government. Budget efficiency is used as an indicator by the proportion of regional expenditure to

the results show that losr has a negative relationship with the estimated state losses from corruption. this indicates that the fiscal independence of the regions might have a positive effect on the level of accountability and the reduction of the risk of corruption. one of the possible explanations is that the regions that have a high level of losr have the potential to invest in the development of the administrative and technological infrastructure of the financial management system. this will lead to the improvement of the level of transparency in the management of public finance through the development of digital governance and e-government (Brinkerhoff & Brinkerhoff, 2015). Moreover, the fiscal independence of the regions will allow the government to invest in the development of the human resource of the country, which will lead to the improvement of the level of supervision of the financial management system (grindle, 2007). this study has different results from several studies that have indicated that fiscal independence increases the level of corruption (Altunbaş & thornton, 2012; lessmann & Markwardt, 2010; Maria et al., 2021; ratmono et al., 2021). 

conversely, balance funds display a positive relationship with corruption. this suggests that the greater the utilization of intergovernmental transfers, the greater the likelihood of corruption in local government. this is due to the complex process involved in the allocation and distribution of balance funds that could lead to rent-seeking behavior and political lobbying (Maria & gudono, 2017). While the allocation process is governed by a set of regulations stipulated in the government regulation number 55 of 2005, the process itself could be subject to information asymmetry between the central government and local government. this is in line with the agency theory that suggests that information asymmetry could lead to a conflict of interest between government officials and the government, which could result in opportunistic behavior. this is in line with the results of previous studies that highlighted the corruption risks involved in the utilization of large amounts of intergovernmental transfers (Maria et al., 2019; ratmono et al., 2021). 

the relationship between budget efficiency and corruption indicates that the findings show that the higher budget efficiency significantly reduces corruption losses. the initial perception of such findings might seem paradoxical, considering that the higher the budget efficiency, the lower the level of corruption. the relationship might indicate the presence of the so-called transparency paradox in public governance. indeed, the higher the budget efficiency, the higher the level of auditing, the higher the level of detail in the budgeting procedures, and the higher the level of financial reporting (Kaufmann et al., 2010). the improvement of the monitoring procedures might indicate that the higher the level of such procedures, the higher the level of detected corruption, rather than the actual level of corruption. the same arguments are presented in the study on public sector governance, showing that the higher the level of transparency, the higher the level of detected corruption (olken, 2007).

## _**5.4.3. Budget composition and corruption**_ 

the nature of government expenditures is an important aspect of government fiscal behavior, whereby the incentives for corruption differ depending on the type of government expenditures. the nature of government expenditures in decentralized governments, where personnel and capital expenditures are important aspects of government spending, can have a significant effect on administrative behavior and monitoring complexities. the above aspects of government fiscal behavior can be important for analyzing the effects of fiscal behavior on corruption. 

these findings reveal that there is a positive relationship between personnel expenditure and estimated state losses from corruption. this implies that an increase in personnel expenditure can lead to an increase in corruption in local bureaucracies. it is worth noting that personnel expenditure is usually a significant component of the budget in local governments, involving intricate administrative processes in handling matters such as salaries, allowances, employment, and promotions. this situation makes it easier for bureaucrats to engage in rent-seeking behavior, especially in environments characterized by low levels of administrative oversight or high levels of administrative discretion (changwony & paterson, 2019; shon & cho, 2020). it is worth mentioning that in indonesia, personnel expenditure has been a significant component of the budget in local governments, at times even exceeding the suggested limits in terms of fiscal sustainability. 

contrary to this, capital expenditure is found to be negatively correlated with corruption. this indicates that investing more in the development of the overall infrastructure may help in reducing the level of corruption. the reason for this may be the fact that the scope of capital expenditure is more closely monitored and regulated. For example, when developing the overall infrastructure of the country, including the development of roads and bridges, more formal procedures are followed, and documentation is required. in this case, the scope of the misuse of funds is more closely monitored and regulated. such factors have also been discussed in the context of public financial management, where it is highlighted that investing in the overall capital of the country is closely monitored and regulated (campos & pradhan, 2007; liu, 2016). 

Another reason may be associated with learning and administrative capacity. As local governments undertake a greater number of capital projects, their ability to plan and manage these projects may improve. this can enhance internal controls and limit technical inefficiencies in public spending (campos & pradhan, 2007). Finally, infrastructure projects may be more salient to the public. this can enhance public monitoring and participation in oversight processes (Fox, 2015). the salience of infrastructure projects may be associated with an informal accountability mechanism that discourages corrupt behavior. 

however, as revealed by the moderation analysis, governance does not significantly affect the relationship between personnel expenditure, capital expenditure, and corruption. this reveals that the effectiveness of governance might be subject to certain institutional or contextual factors, as opposed to expenditure variables. governance interventions usually cover a wide range of aspects, including transparency, institutional capacity, accountability, and regulations, among others. it is, therefore, plausible that these aspects might not affect expenditure variables (rothstein & teorell, 2008). the effectiveness of governance might be compromised by differences in policy implementation at local levels in decentralized systems like indonesia (Andrews et al., 2017).

For example, the macro-economic environment, the politico-institutional dynamics, and the reforms implemented at various levels of governance can all have implications for the implementation and monitoring of fiscal policies (Acemoglu & robinson, 2021). Furthermore, the differences in the incentive and remuneration structures of the bureaucracy in various parts of the country may have implications for the behavior of the administration that cannot be captured by governance indicators (Mungiu-pippidi, 2015). these factors point towards the fact that while governance is an important factor in the institutional environment, the moderating role of governance in the case of expenditure-related corruption risks may be mediated by the overall politico-economic environment. 

## _**5.4.4. Intergovernmental transfers and corruption**_ 

in this case, intergovernmental fiscal transfer is an integral part of the fiscal decentralization system, especially in nations whose local governments depend on funding from the central government to support service delivery. the balance funds in indonesia is intended to minimize fiscal gaps between different regions and provide local governments with sufficient funding to deliver public services. however, this could also affect corruption risks depending on how the funding is allocated and used. 

From the results, it is evident that the general Allocation Fund (gAF) does not have a statistically significant impact on corruption. it is worth mentioning that the general Allocation Fund is intended to facilitate fiscal equalization between different regions in the country. however, there exist several factors that could affect the overall impact of the general Allocation Fund on corruption. First, the allocation of the general Allocation Fund is primarily formula-driven and is allocated to several regions. it is expected that the differences in the financial management capacities of local governments could affect the overall impact of the general Allocation Fund allocation on corruption. secondly, the utilization of the general Allocation Fund is flexible and not tied to any development projects. this could make it difficult to identify the relationship between the utilization of the general Allocation Fund and corruption. Finally, the absence of detailed reporting structures for the utilization of the general Allocation Fund could make it difficult to identify the overall state losses that could be attributed to corruption involving the general Allocation Fund (smoke, 2015). 

on the other hand, the specific Allocation Fund (sAF) is negatively associated with corruption. this indicates that the application of specific allocation funds could minimize the level of corruption. the specific allocation funds are often allocated for specific development needs such as infrastructure development, education, and health. the application of these funds for specific development needs is often subject to rigorous monitoring and evaluation processes. the documentation of projects and the reporting of the performance of the projects could enhance transparency and thus help in the detection of any form of corruption in the application of the funds (olken, 2007). the projects that are developed using the specific allocation funds are often visible in the community. the community could thus play an important role in the minimization of corruption in the application of the specific allocation funds. 

Moreover, the results suggest that the revenue sharing Fund (rsF) is positively related to corruption. revenue-sharing systems usually involve the allocation of revenues collected from natural resources or other central government revenues to local governments. While such allocation is intended to compensate the local governments that produce the revenues and to support local development, it could also be related to a higher risk of corruption when dealing with large amounts of financial resources with limited institutional monitoring. previous studies highlighted the fact that large amounts of financial resources could generate the incentive for rent-seeking behavior among political and bureaucratic actors (rose-Ackerman, 1999; svensson, 2005). in decentralized political systems, local government officials could also be subject to the need to allocate these resources through patronage networks. 

the moderation analysis demonstrates that governance mitigates the relationship between gAF and sAF and corruption. the result suggests that better governance structures have the potential to mitigate risks associated with corruption in the transfer of finances. When effective governance structures are in place, the monitoring systems are more effective in detecting any anomalies, and there is greater pressure on public servants to account for their actions (olken & pande, 2012). Better governance structures have the potential to engage the public more effectively in monitoring the budget and to enhance the capacity of local governments in managing public finances effectively (Fung, 2015; smoke, 2015).

however, the role of governance seems to enhance the relationship between rsF and corruption. this may be due to the weaknesses in the monitoring of revenue-sharing funds. generally, in decentralized systems, the revenues from natural resources and revenue-sharing transfers have complex financial flows and many administrative actors, which may hinder the monitoring of the funds. the lack of transparency in the distribution of revenues and revenue-sharing transfers, as well as the lack of public participation in the monitoring of these funds, may hinder the role of governance in the prevention of corruption in the revenue-sharing transfers. thus, despite the implementation of governance, the level of corruption in revenue-sharing transfers may be high. 

## _**5.4.5. The influence of control variables on corruption**_ 

several control variables were included in the analysis. these variables controlled for various structural and institutional factors that might affect the risk of corruption in different local governments. they accounted for differences in demographic, bureaucratic, economic, and institutional factors that might affect the environment in which fiscal decentralization takes place. 

the results suggest that the population size has a positive relationship with corruption. it is expected that the larger the population, the greater the complexity of government administration and the greater the demand for public services. With the increase in the complexity of government administration, the easier it becomes for the government activities to go undetected, which in turn increases the possibility of corruption (shleifer & vishny, 1993; treisman, 2000). high rates of population growth can put pressure on public service delivery and government budgets. 

similarly, the figure of civil servants reveals a positive correlation with corruption. this is because, as bureaucratic size increases, administrative complexities may also rise, making it even more difficult to monitor. consequently, as the size of the public sector workforce increases, so does the figure of individuals involve in decision-making processes. this, in turn, may lead to rent-seeking behavior (rose-Ackerman, 1999). previous studies have also pointed out that, in large bureaucratic systems, inadequate incentives and monitoring may lead to a rise in corruption risks (Brunetti & Weder, 2003). 

gross regional Domestic product (grDp), on the other hand, is positively associated with corruption. the higher the level of economic activities in the region, the higher the level of economic development, which might translate into higher investments in the region, particularly for development projects. Although economic growth might provide the necessary financial resources for the local government units, economic development might also provide the opportunities for corruption (Mauro, 1995). the large development projects that require huge investments from the public sector have been identified as potential sources of corruption risk in public finance management (Kenny, 2007). 

in contrast, the regional autonomy era reveals a negative correlation with corruption. As the regions get older, their administration is likely to become more established, their institutional experience greater, and their system of governance more developed. over time, there may be an improvement in financial management, the regulatory environment, and the capacity for oversight (la porta et al., 1999; treisman, 2000). newer regions may still be in the process of consolidating their administration and may have problems with their system of governance that could give rise to higher levels of corruption. 

regional differences have also played a significant part in the explanation of the differences in the patterns of corruption in indonesia. it has been observed that the estimated costs of corruption in the country have been lower in the regions that are located on the island of Java compared to the regions that are located outside the island. this could be due to the better infrastructure that has been developed in the regions of Java. it has been observed that the regions of Java have better institutional environments, better educational infrastructure, and better proximity to the administrative center of the country. 

Apart from geographic differences, administrative levels, including provinces, regencies, and municipalities, also pose differences in corruption risks. For instance, at the provincial administrative level, there is usually a larger budget and development programs, especially in infrastructure development. the larger budget could mean a larger financial resource under administrative discretion, hence a larger scope of corruption risks (Kenny, 2007; Mustapha, 2014). regencies, on the other hand, also handle large financial resources, especially in areas like education and health, which might expose these administrative levels to corruption risks. At the municipal administrative level, there is usually a smaller budget, hence a smaller administrative structure, which might be easier to monitor.

## _**5.5. Discussion summary and theoretical contribution**_ 

the results of this study collectively reveal the complex institutional relationship between fiscal decentralization, governance, and corruption at the local government level. on one hand, fiscal independence seems to have a positive effect on the strength of accountability mechanisms, while reliance on intergovernmental transfers seems to have the potential for rent-seeking behavior. on the other hand, the effect of governance in the relationship between fiscal decentralization and corruption detection is complex, as it sometimes has a positive effect on the detection of corruption through enhanced monitoring and transparency mechanisms. 

Apart from contributing to the body of knowledge in terms of empirical findings, this study also makes significant contributions to the body of knowledge in terms of fiscal decentralization and corruption in the following ways: Firstly, it recognizes the differential impact of different fiscal tools by differentiating between fiscal independence, dependency, and expenditure efficiency, as opposed to viewing decentralization. secondly, it establishes that governance can affect corruption not only by mitigating it but also by detecting it, as seen in a transparency paradox in governance. thirdly, it offers new knowledge about how corruption works in an indonesian setting of decentralized governance. 

## **6. Conclusion** 

Fiscal decentralization can be a determinant of decreasing the estimated state losses from corruption. specifically, the greater the local own source revenue (losr) and balance funds can increase the estimated state losses from corruption. While for the budget efficiency, does not have a significant impact on reducing the estimated state losses from corruption. the interaction between governance and the three dimensions of fiscal decentralization measurement (losr, balance funds, budget efficiency), proven to strengthen the predicted results according to the hypothesis. these results indicate that the interaction of fiscal decentralization and governance strengthens the decrease in the estimated state losses from corruption. 

Budget measurement efficiency, which was originally a ratio between expenditure and income, has been proven to be able to be replaced by personnel expenditure and capital expenditure. When associated with predicting the estimated state losses from corruption, both substitute measures can provide partial impacts. however, for interactions with government governance, the significance of both substitute measures has not been proven. 

Balance measurement fund, which was originally in the form of total transfer funds from the central government to the regions, has been proven to be able to be replaced with general Allocation Fund (gAF), special Allocation Fund (sAF), and revenue sharing Fund (rsF). When associated to predict the estimated state losses from corruption, only sAF and rsF are proven to have a partial impact. however, if the interaction with government governance is carried out, then all these substitute measures are proven to have a significant impact on reducing the estimated state losses from corruption. these results prove that both gAF, sAF, and rsF cannot stand alone, but rather require governance to provide an optimal impact on reducing corruption. 

## _**6.1. Implications**_ 

the findings of the current study can be used to draw several implications at the theoretical and practical levels. From a theoretical point of view, the current study provides evidence to confirm the applicability of the balance funds as a major indicator of fiscal decentralization, as suggested by the regulatory framework that outlines the concept of regional autonomy in indonesia (law no. 32/2004; law no. 33/2004; pp no. 55/2005). in addition, the current study contributes to the body of literature on public sector accounting by providing a long-term assessment of fiscal decentralization outcomes. Between 2010 and 2021, fiscal decentralization has been effective in reducing the estimated losses to the state from corruption. 

in practical terms, the results underscore the necessity to develop more structured policy mechanisms to reconcile the need for greater fiscal autonomy with anticorruption control. specifically, the central government can enhance risk-based auditing by targeting those regions with high rates of fiscal growth, procurement complexity, or weaker internal control systems. second, the scope of conditional intergovernmental transfers can be expanded by linking a greater share of the balance funds to various

Fourth, capacity building can be targeted for those regions with weaker levels of administrative capacity, with a focus on budgeting, procurement, and internal control systems. lastly, with respect to the policy for expanding the scope of the regions, an assessment of good governance dimensions, including the sustainability of the losr system, balance funds dependency, and the competencies of the human resources base, should be integrated to ensure that the newly expanded local governments can operate autonomously without exacerbating the risk of corruption. 

## _**6.2. Limitations and future research**_ 

this research recognizes that there are several limitations that need to be taken into consideration when interpreting the results and designing future studies. First, there is the issue of data access. there is a considerable waiting period required to obtain financial reports and transfer funds data from the Ministry of home Affairs, as well as estimates of loss due to corruption and audit results from the supreme Audit institution of indonesia (BpK). secondly, despite the successful request process, there is limited availability of data because not all archives of the local government have been fully incorporated into the central databases of the Ministry of home Affairs or the BpK. third, the issue of measuring the level of corruption and the level of governance is problematic. corruption cannot be quantitatively defined, but the best proxy for measuring it is the estimated value of the losses, whereas the global level is limited to perception indicators or _inkracht_ cases. governance cannot be quantitatively defined, but it can be proxied by the results of internal control, whereas the government’s comprehensive index of regional governance (itKpD) is still at the stage of development, limited to the period of 2022–2023. 

Future research may address the above-mentioned shortcomings in the following ways. to address the data access issue, it is suggested that future research should allow for appropriate lead time, ranging from 6 to 12 months, for institutional data requests. As for the problem of absent data, it is possible to use the alternative method of directly accessing the data from the websites of the local governments, though the regularity of such updates is still inconsistent. to address the problem of measurements, it is possible to use alternative research methods, such as narrower case studies, which would allow for more detailed research of the patterns of corruption for a limited number of regions during a limited period. As the itKpD index is still evolving, future research may benefit from the use of this emerging index of governance.
